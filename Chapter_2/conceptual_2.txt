QUESTION 1: FLEXIBILITY OF STATISTICAL LEARNING METHODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
For each of parts (a) through (d), indicate whether we would generally expect 
the performance of a flexible statistical learning method to be better or 
worse than an inflexible method. Justify your answer.

(a) The sample size n is extremely large, and the number of predictors p is 
    small.
    - Performance: Generally better.
    - Justification: Flexible methods, particularly non-parametric ones, 
      require a very large number of observations to obtain an accurate 
      estimate of the function f. When the sample size is extremely large, the 
      flexible model has enough data to accurately fit the underlying 
      functional form without being as prone to overfitting.

(b) The number of predictors p is extremely large, and the number of 
    observations n is small.
    - Performance: Generally worse.
    - Justification: A flexible method would likely lead to overfitting the 
      data, meaning it follows the random errors or "noise" too closely rather 
      than the systematic relationship. Because flexible models must estimate 
      more parameters or complex shapes without enough data points to guide 
      them, they produce high variance, leading to poor performance on new 
      test observations.

(c) The relationship between the predictors and response is highly non-linear.
    - Performance: Generally better.
    - Justification: Inflexible methods, such as linear regression, introduce 
      high bias when the true relationship is non-linear because they assume a 
      shape (like a straight line) that does not match reality. Flexible 
      methods are specifically designed to accurately fit a wider range of 
      possible shapes for f and can capture curvature that inflexible models 
      would miss.

(d) The variance of the error terms, i.e., variance of epsilon, is extremely 
    high.
    - Performance: Generally worse.
    - Justification: When the variance of the error terms (the irreducible 
      error) is high, the data contains a significant amount of "noise." 
      Flexible methods are particularly susceptible to picking up these random 
      patterns that do not actually exist in the true function. An inflexible 
      model is often preferable in high-noise environments because it is less 
      likely to overfit the errors and provides a more stable, lower-variance 
      estimate.


QUESTION 2: CLASSIFICATION VS. REGRESSION & INFERENCE VS. PREDICTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Explain whether each scenario is a classification or regression problem, and 
indicate whether we are most interested in inference or prediction. Finally, 
provide n and p.

(a) We collect a set of data on the top 500 firms in the US. For each firm, we 
    record profit, number of employees, industry, and the CEO salary. We are 
    interested in understanding which factors affect CEO salary.
    - Problem Type: Regression. The response variable, CEO salary, is a 
      quantitative (numerical) value.
    - Goal: Inference. The primary interest is "understanding which factors 
      affect" the salary, which involves identifying associated predictors and 
      the nature of their relationship with the response.
    - Sample Size (n): 500.
    - Number of Predictors (p): 3 (profit, number of employees, and industry).

(b) We are considering launching a new product and wish to know whether it will 
    be a success or a failure. We collect data on 20 similar products that were 
    previously launched. For each product, we have recorded whether it was a 
    success or failure, price charged for the product, marketing budget, 
    competition price, and ten other variables.
    - Problem Type: Classification. The response is "success or failure," 
      which is a qualitative (categorical) variable.
    - Goal: Prediction. The objective is to determine whether a future product 
      will succeed or fail based on past data.
    - Sample Size (n): 20.
    - Number of Predictors (p): 13.

(c) We are interested in predicting the % change in the USD/Euro exchange rate 
    in relation to the weekly changes in the world stock markets. Hence we 
    collect weekly data for all of 2012. For each week we record the % change 
    in the USD/Euro, the % change in the US market, the % change in the British 
    market, and the % change in the German market.
    - Problem Type: Regression. The response variable is the "% change in the 
      USD/Euro exchange rate," which is a quantitative value.
    - Goal: Prediction. The focus is explicitly on "predicting the % change" 
      in the rate rather than understanding the underlying structural 
      associations.
    - Sample Size (n): 52 (weekly recordings for the entire year of 2012).
    - Number of Predictors (p): 3.


QUESTION 3: THE BIAS-VARIANCE DECOMPOSITION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
(a) Provide a sketch description of typical curves as we go from less flexible 
    statistical learning methods towards more flexible approaches. 

    The plot features flexibility on the x-axis (increasing left to right) and 
    error/variance/bias on the y-axis.
    - Squared Bias: Starts high at low flexibility and monotonically decreases 
      as flexibility increases.
    - Variance: Starts low at low flexibility and monotonically increases as 
      flexibility increases.
    - Training Error (MSE): Monotonically declines as flexibility increases, 
      typically staying below the test error curve.
    - Test Error (MSE): A U-shaped curve that initially decreases, reaches a 
      minimum point, and then increases again.
    - Bayes (Irreducible) Error: A horizontal dashed line that remains 
      constant across all levels of flexibility.

(b) Explain why each of the five curves has the shape displayed in part (a).
    - Squared Bias: Bias refers to the error introduced by approximating a 
      complex real-life problem with a simpler model. Inflexible models have 
      high bias. As flexibility increases, the model can fit a wider range of 
      shapes, decreasing the bias.
    - Variance: Variance refers to how much the estimated function would change 
      if a different training dataset were used. Inflexible models are stable 
      (low variance). Highly flexible models follow observations closely; 
      changing one point significantly changes the estimate (high variance).
    - Training Error: Models are designed to minimize training MSE. Increased 
      flexibility allows the model to follow data points more closely, 
      resulting in a constant decline in the training error.
    - Test Error: The expected test MSE is the sum of variance, squared bias, 
      and irreducible error. As flexibility increases, bias initially drops 
      faster than variance rises (test error decreases). Eventually, the 
      increase in variance outweighs the decrease in bias, causing test error 
      to rise (overfitting).
    - Bayes (Irreducible) Error: Associated with the random error term epsilon. 
      It contains unmeasured variation that cannot be predicted. It is 
      independent of flexibility, creating a constant floor.


QUESTION 4: REAL-LIFE APPLICATIONS FOR STATISTICAL LEARNING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
(a) Classification Applications (Qualitative/Categorical Response)
    1. Medical Diagnosis:
       - Response: Patient's risk for a severe adverse drug reaction 
         (High/Low) or cancer diagnosis.
       - Predictors: Clinical measurements (blood sample characteristics, 
         weight, blood pressure, age, family history).
       - Goal: Prediction (accurately classifying risk).
    2. Direct Marketing:
       - Response: Whether an individual responds positively or negatively 
         to a campaign.
       - Predictors: Demographic variables.
       - Goal: Prediction (identifying likely responders).
    3. Consumer Brand Choice:
       - Response: The brand a customer purchases (Brand A, B, or C).
       - Predictors: Product price, store location, discount levels, 
         competition price.
       - Goal: Inference (understanding price sensitivity).

(b) Regression Applications (Quantitative/Numerical Response)
    1. Advertising and Sales:
       - Response: Sales of a particular product.
       - Predictors: Advertising budgets (TV, radio, newspaper).
       - Goal: Inference (identifying which media boost sales).
    2. Real Estate Valuation:
       - Response: The value or price of a home.
       - Predictors: Crime rate, income level, size, air quality.
       - Goal: Both (Predicting house value and inferring feature impacts).
    3. Stock Market Analysis:
       - Response: Price of a stock or % change in an exchange rate.
       - Predictors: Past stock returns or changes in world markets.
       - Goal: Prediction (accuracy for financial trading).

(c) Cluster Analysis Applications (Unsupervised)
    1. Market Segmentation: Grouping customers by shopping habits to 
       identify segments for targeted marketing.
    2. Biological Discovery: Grouping tissue samples to identify unknown 
       disease subtypes.
    3. Data Organization: Automated clustering to identify hidden patterns 
       in large datasets where visual inspection is impossible.


QUESTION 5: FLEXIBLE VS. LESS FLEXIBLE APPROACHES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. Very Flexible Approaches
    - Advantages: They do not make explicit assumptions about the functional 
      form, allowing them to fit complex shapes. They result in less bias.
    - Disadvantages: Highly susceptible to overfitting (high variance). They 
      generally have low interpretability and require a large number of 
      observations.

2. Less Flexible (Restrictive) Approaches
    - Advantages: Highly interpretable. They have low variance (stable) and 
      perform well even with small sample sizes.
    - Disadvantages: High bias because they assume a simple functional form 
      that may fail to capture non-linear patterns.

3. When to Prefer a More Flexible Approach:
    - Highly Non-linear Relationships.
    - Large Sample Sizes.
    - Prediction is the Goal.

4. When to Prefer a Less Flexible Approach:
    - Linear Relationships.
    - Small Sample Sizes.
    - Inference is the Goal.
    - High Noise Environments.


QUESTION 6: PARAMETRIC VS. NON-PARAMETRIC APPROACHES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Differences Between the Approaches:
    - Parametric Methods: A two-step approach. First, an assumption is made 
      about the functional form. Second, the model is trained by estimating 
      parameters based on the data.
    - Non-Parametric Methods: Do not make explicit assumptions about the 
      functional form. They seek an estimate that stays close to the data 
      without becoming excessively "wiggly."

Advantages of Parametric Approaches:
    - Simplification: Reduces the problem to estimating a small set of 
      numerical coefficients.
    - Lower Data Requirements: Typically require fewer observations.
    - Interpretability: Restrictive models clearly show which predictors are 
      associated with the response.

Disadvantages of Parametric Approaches:
    - Model Mismatch: The assumed form will usually not perfectly match 
      the true form.
    - Bias: Can suffer from high bias when the relationship is complex.
    - Risk of Overfitting: Using complex parametric models can lead to 
      overfitting the noise.


QUESTION 7: K-NEAREST NEIGHBORS (KNN) CALCULATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Obs. | X1 | X2 | X3 | Y
1    | 0  | 3  | 0  | Red
2    | 2  | 0  | 0  | Red
3    | 0  | 1  | 3  | Red
4    | 0  | 1  | 2  | Green
5    | -1 | 0  | 1  | Green
6    | 1  | 1  | 1  | Red

(a) Euclidean Distance Calculation (to X1=X2=X3=0)
    Obs. 1: sqrt(0^2 + 3^2 + 0^2) = 3
    Obs. 2: sqrt(2^2 + 0^2 + 0^2) = 2
    Obs. 3: sqrt(0^2 + 1^2 + 3^2) = sqrt(10) ~ 3.16
    Obs. 4: sqrt(0^2 + 1^2 + 2^2) = sqrt(5)  ~ 2.24
    Obs. 5: sqrt((-1)^2 + 0^2 + 1^2) = sqrt(2) ~ 1.41
    Obs. 6: sqrt(1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2) = sqrt(3)  ~ 1.73

(b) Prediction with K=1
    - Prediction: Green.
    - Why: The closest point is Obs. 5 (distance 1.41), which is Green.

(c) Prediction with K=3
    - Prediction: Red.
    - Why: The three closest are Obs. 5 (Green), Obs. 6 (Red), and 
      Obs. 2 (Red). The majority class is Red.

(d) Optimal K for a Highly Non-linear Boundary
    - Expected K: Small.
    - Why: A small K makes the model highly flexible, allowing the decision 
      boundary to capture complex patterns. As K increases, the boundary 
      becomes smoother (less flexible).
